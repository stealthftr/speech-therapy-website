Is this your child?

You've noticed something. That's usually the most important part.

Parents are almost always the first to sense when something's up with their child's talking — long before any test. Below are the concerns we hear most, what each one often means, and what a good next step looks like. You don't need to know which one fits. That's our job.

Often 12 months – 3 years

Not talking yet, or barely

Few or no words when you'd expect more, lots of pointing and grunting, or a vocabulary that just isn't growing. This is the single most common reason families come to Lark — and the area where an early start makes the biggest difference.

  • No words by around 15–16 months
  • Fewer than ~50 words, or no two-word combos, by age 2
  • Points and grunts instead of trying words
Talk to us about your late talker
Often 2 – 8 years

Hard to understand

You translate for your child; strangers say "sorry, what?"; sounds get dropped, swapped or muddled. When you're your child's only interpreter, ordinary days get exhausting for both of you. Clarity is very treatable, and we make the practice feel like a game.

  • Strangers understand less than you'd expect for their age
  • Drops or swaps sounds beyond the usual age
  • Gives up mid-sentence, or gets cross being asked to repeat
Get an honest read on their clarity
Often 2 – 6 years

Getting stuck & stuttering

Repeating sounds ("b-b-ball"), stretching them out, or pushing hard to get words out — sometimes with a little tension in the face or body. Some bumpy speech is a normal phase; some benefits from early support. We help you tell the difference and build confident communicators.

  • Repeats or gets "stuck" for more than ~6 months
  • Visible effort, tension or frustration when talking
  • Starts avoiding talking, or says "I can't say it"
Is it a phase? Let's find out
Any age

Not following or understanding

Instructions need repeating; your child seems to "tune out" language, struggles to answer questions, or gets lost in a conversation. Understanding language is just as important as speaking it — and it's core speech-pathology work.

  • Trouble following directions without gestures or repeats
  • Doesn't answer simple questions, or answers off-topic
  • A teacher has mentioned "receptive language" or "processing"
Talk it through with us
Often 5 – 12 years

School, reading & making friends

They can talk, but conversations are one-sided, jokes fall flat, or friendships are hard. Or they read the words but miss the story, and struggle to retell what happened at school. These language skills quietly underpin school and social life — and they're teachable.

  • Struggles with conversation, turn-taking or reading the room
  • Reads words but doesn't understand the story
  • Can't retell what happened, or gets the order muddled
Talk through school concerns

Please don't "wait and see" — reach out promptly if you notice:

  • Going backwards: losing words, sounds or social skills they used to have — at any age. Mention this to your GP too.
  • Not responding to sound: possible hearing concerns deserve a hearing check straight away.
  • Deep frustration: when not being understood is causing real distress for your child (or your family) day after day.
