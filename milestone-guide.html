Is my child's talking on track?

Age-by-age communication milestones and the signs worth a chat, from a speech pathologist. Find your child's age band below. Every child develops at their own pace — one missing item is usually nothing; a pattern is your cue to get a professional opinion.

Birth–12 mo

Coos, babbles & first words

What most babies do

  • Startles at loud sounds; turns toward your voice
  • Coos and giggles by ~4 months; babbles ("bababa") by ~7 months
  • Uses gestures — waving, reaching, pointing — by ~10–12 months
  • Responds to their name; enjoys peek-a-boo
  • Says a first word or two ("mum," "uh-oh") around 12 months

Worth a chat if…

  • Doesn't react to sounds or voices, or doesn't turn to their name by 9 months
  • No babbling by 9 months; a very quiet baby
  • No gestures (pointing, waving) by 12 months
12–18 mo

First words take off

What most toddlers do

  • Says 5–20+ words by 18 months (approximations like "ba" for ball count!)
  • Points to show you things and looks back at you
  • Follows simple directions like "get your shoes"
  • Understands far more than they can say

Worth a chat if…

  • No words by 15–16 months
  • Doesn't point, wave, or use other gestures
  • Doesn't seem to understand simple, familiar words ("cup," "bye-bye")
  • Loses words or skills they used to have — mention to your GP promptly
18–24 mo

The word explosion

What most toddlers do

  • Vocabulary grows fast — 50+ words by age 2
  • Starts combining two words: "more milk," "daddy go"
  • Points to body parts and pictures in books when asked
  • Familiar adults understand them about half the time

Worth a chat if…

  • Fewer than ~50 words, or no two-word combinations, by 24 months — the classic "late talker" profile, and the most common reason families come to us
  • Mostly grunts and points instead of trying words
  • Big frustration or meltdowns around communicating
2–3 yrs

Sentences & stories begin

What most kids do

  • Uses 2–4 word phrases and asks simple questions
  • Follows two-step directions ("get the book and bring it to me")
  • Strangers understand about half to three-quarters of their speech by 3
  • Some sound swaps ("wabbit") are still completely normal

Worth a chat if…

  • Not combining words into short phrases by 2½
  • Even you struggle to understand your own child by age 3
  • Repeating sounds/syllables with visible tension, or lasting beyond ~6 months
  • Doesn't respond to simple questions or seems to "tune out" language
3–4 yrs

Conversation takes shape

What most kids do

  • Speaks in 4+ word sentences; tells simple stories about their day
  • Asks "why?" constantly (sorry)
  • Strangers understand most of what they say
  • Plays and talks with other children

Worth a chat if…

  • Speech is still hard for unfamiliar listeners to understand
  • Sentences stay very short or jumbled; leaves out little words
  • Trouble following directions without gestures or repetition
  • Avoids talking, especially outside the home
4–5 yrs

Ready for school

What most kids do

  • Tells longer stories that mostly stay on topic
  • Nearly all speech sounds are clear (a lingering R, L or TH is still common)
  • Uses grammar close to adult patterns; answers "when/how/why" questions
  • Plays cooperatively; understood by nearly everyone

Worth a chat if…

  • Multiple speech sounds still unclear; classmates ask "what did they say?"
  • Trouble retelling events in order, learning letters/rhymes, or following classroom directions
  • Ongoing stuttering, especially with tension, blinking or avoidance
  • A teacher has raised a concern — take it seriously (and calmly)

Please don't "wait and see" — reach out promptly if you notice:

  • Going backwards: losing words, sounds or social skills they used to have — at any age. Mention to your GP too.
  • No response to sound: possible hearing concerns deserve a hearing check straight away — ask your GP or an audiologist.
  • Real distress: when not being understood is causing ongoing frustration for your child or family.

The honest truth about "wait and see"

Some children genuinely do catch up on their own — research on late talkers suggests many will. The catch: no one can reliably tell you in advance which children catch up and which fall further behind. Meanwhile, the youngest brains respond fastest to support, and waitlists in many places run for months.

So here's the rule of thumb we'd give a friend: "wait and see" is fine when there are no signs above and your gut is quiet. If there's a sign — or your gut won't settle — have a chat. It's quick, it's free here, and the worst case is reassurance. No one regrets checking too early. Plenty of parents tell us they wish they'd checked sooner.
